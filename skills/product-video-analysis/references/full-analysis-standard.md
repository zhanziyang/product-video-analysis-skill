# Full Analysis Standard

A complete report contains all 25 modules below. New modules may be added, but none may be removed or collapsed into a vague summary.

## 1. Product background and core value

State what the product is, who it serves, and which facts are verified. Explain which product characteristics matter to the video.

**Minimum evidence:** official source or explicit inability-to-verify note.

## 2. Communication objective

Define the audience state before and after viewing: what should they understand, feel, remember, or do?

## 3. Creative proposition

Reduce the video's idea to one sentence. Distinguish the proposition from the tagline and from the feature list.

## 4. Planning logic

Explain why the chosen format, reveal order, abstraction level, and proof style fit the communication problem.

## 5. Full narrative structure

Divide the entire duration into acts with time ranges, dramatic function, information payload, and transition logic.

## 6. Copy architecture

Transcribe or summarize copy in sequence. Identify setup, contrast, escalation, pivot words, proof, reveal, and close.

## 7. Music, sound effects, and voice-over

Map music sections, VO phrases, UI clicks, impacts, risers, whooshes, textures, and silence to specific events.

## 8. Tempo and motion-density map

Show BPM candidates, perceived pulse, event density, rests, peaks, and how pacing changes across the story.

## 9. Timecoded screenshot breakdown

Cover the whole film. Fast actions need before/middle/after or denser sequences; quiet holds may use fewer frames.

## 10. Animation terminology

Name every important motion using common industry vocabulary. Use combined names when necessary, such as “masked push + scale overshoot.”

## 11. Property changes

Describe transform, opacity, blur, mask, path, tracking, color, 3D rotation, camera, depth, and compositing changes.

## 12. Estimated duration and frame count

Give seconds and frames at the source frame rate. State uncertainty when sampling is sparse.

## 13. Timing function and graph shape

Classify ease-in/out, ease-out, linear, exponential, back, elastic, spring, hold, stepped, or custom. Provide an approximate cubic-bezier only where meaningful.

## 14. Motion principles

Identify anticipation, overshoot, bounce, follow-through, overlap, squash/stretch, staging, arcs, secondary motion, and settle.

## 15. Composition, typography, color, and depth

Analyze hierarchy, alignment, negative space, scale contrast, reading order, font behavior, palette, lighting, and spatial layers.

## 16. Transition system

Explain recurring transition grammar: match cuts, object continuity, zoom-through, wipes, morphs, hard cuts, defocus, directional continuity, or audio bridges.

## 17. Motion-to-product mapping

For each key motion, state which product quality it expresses: speed, control, flexibility, intelligence, trust, creativity, scale, calmness, precision, or personalization.

## 18. Copy-image-motion-sound coordination

Use a table showing what each channel contributes at the same moment. Point out redundancy, reinforcement, and productive contrast.

## 19. Implementation methods

Provide plausible After Effects, Figma, 3D, editing, Rive/Lottie, CSS, or WebGL methods. Separate likely original technique from recommended recreation technique.

## 20. Project organization

Recommend compositions, precomps, naming, frame-rate strategy, adjustment layers, audio tracks, proxies, color management, and export settings.

## 21. Simplified recreation

Design a version a learner can complete with fewer assets while preserving the central idea and motion grammar.

## 22. Staged training plan

Break practice into small exercises ordered by dependency. Each exercise has an output, constraint, and evaluation criterion.

## 23. Transferable methodology

Extract rules that generalize beyond the source video. Avoid advice that is merely “copy this visual.”

## 24. Application to the user's product

Translate the method to the user's actual product characteristics, copy, feature order, visual metaphor, sound palette, and feasible production scope.

## 25. Continuous-frame appendix

Include a contact sheet or regular sample sequence covering the entire duration, plus denser inserts for important motion.

## Coverage rules

- A module counts only when it contains concrete analysis, not a heading with one generic sentence.
- The animation modules require shot-level evidence.
- Long videos may group repeated patterns, but the full narrative timeline still covers the entire duration.
- A report may be delivered in parts only when every part clearly states which modules and time ranges remain incomplete.
